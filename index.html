Field site

2.1.a Study area

The study is conducted at the Awajishima Monkey Center (AMC) near Sumoto City on Awaji Island (Hyōgo Prefecture), Japan—a coastal, forested hillside site overlooking Osaka Bay. AMC is a privately operated tourism and education facility hosting a single large group of approximately 400 free-ranging Japanese macaques (Macaca fuscata).

The group is routinely provisioned (typically twice daily) with foods such as wheat, soybeans, peanuts, fruit, and sweet potatoes, and visitors can offer food from within a designated enclosure. Reliance on provisioning varies seasonally, with reduced visits during periods when wild foods are abundant. Because food is provisioned and easier to access, individuals can spend less time foraging and more time available for social behaviours like play, grooming, and maintaining relationships.

2.1.b Study population

The AMC macaques provide an unusual opportunity to examine how physical disability shapes early social life under naturalistic social conditions. Congenital limb malformations (CLM) have been documented in this population for decades, with prevalence estimates reaching ~17% in this free-ranging group. Provisioning likely reduces foraging time and energetic costs, which can increase the time available for social behaviours.

Prior work suggests that many affected individuals develop compensatory strategies to maintain access to resources and social partners. A key open question is whether these compensations fully mitigate the social costs of impairment during development, especially in fast-moving, contact-dependent interactions such as play and other tactile exchanges.

What this project does

Field-first behavioural data

Over a 6-week field season at the Awajishima Monkey Center, I collect continuous focal-follow 4K video of juvenile macaques to quantify activity budgets, play behaviours, play partners, and initiation/response sequences in disabled and non-disabled juveniles.

AI-assisted extraction at scale

From recordings, I extract key frames and short clips, build per-individual photo catalogues, and apply structured behaviour labels (e.g., chase, soft wrestling, solicitation) to support model training and evaluation.

From video → timelines → networks

Using transfer learning, I fine-tune computer vision models to detect macaque faces/bodies, link detections into tracks, identify individuals, and classify major play types—producing individual-level behavioural timelines and interaction datasets.

  • Ground-truth validation: model outputs are compared against human-annotated labels, iteratively refined, then batch-applied to remaining videos.
  • Inference outputs: time-stamped tracklets, play events, partners, and initiation/response sequences.
  • Analysis: mixed-effects models test how disability status, sex, and age predict play rate/type and response patterns; social network metrics quantify partner preferences and social integration.

How it works

Vision pipeline

SAM3-style segmentation + detection → tracklets under occlusion, variable lighting, and dense groups in the provisioning area.

Annotation + ethogram

Per-individual photo catalogues and behaviour labels (e.g., chase, soft wrestle, solicitation) stored as a consistent CSV schema.

Research outputs

Individual-level behavioural timelines + “who→whom” interaction events for mixed-effects models and social network metrics.

  1. Collect: continuous focal-follow 4K video during a 6-week field season (juvenile macaques; disabled vs non-disabled).
  2. Curate: extract key frames + short clips; build per-individual photo catalogues; define a consistent ethogram + annotation schema.
  3. Segment & detect: apply SAM3-style segmentation and/or detectors to isolate individuals under occlusion and variable field conditions.
  4. Track & ID: link detections into tracklets and identify individuals using visual signatures (transfer learning + iterative QA).
  5. Classify play: label major play types and initiation/response patterns; validate against human ground-truth.
  6. Export & analyze: generate individual-level behavioural timelines and interaction tables for mixed-effects models and social network analyses.

The goal isn’t to remove human judgement—it’s to reduce the hours spent on repetitive logging so behavioural nuance can be checked, interpreted, and analyzed.

What the pipeline produces

  • Tracklets: per-individual trajectories through short sequences
  • Play events: candidate bouts with timestamps + partners
  • Initiation/response: structured sequences for behavioural timelines and maps of social networks
  • Exports: CSV tables ready for mixed-effects + network analysis

This is the analysis-ready layer that connects field video to behavioural inference. I'll be able to analyze if the model outlines the body's correctly, if it keeps the same individual labeled consistently across frames/time, if the system can detect when play happens (and when it doesn't), and if this actually reduces workload.
